Early Prenatal Care in Maryland Post-ACA

by LUCY MAYNARD
When-or if-expecting mothers get prenatal care impacts the future health of their children.

College Park, Md.-Two yellow houses off Baltimore Avenue offer hope to uninsured pregnant women. These houses are owned and operated by Pregnancy Aid Centers, Inc. (PAC), a non-profit women's health clinic. Maternal and newborn care, which includes early prenatal care, is considered an essential health benefit under the Affordable Care Act. This means pregant women can enroll in or change their health coverage at any time without penalties. But the percentage of women receiving early prenatal care has only increased one percent between 2011 (82 percent) and 2013 (83 percent). Organizations such as PAC are still needed for some pregnant women to get early prenatal care.

Nine Months of Insurance

A survey conducted by the Maryland Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System (PRAMS) found 20.5 percent of women were uninsured before pregnancy. A majority of the surveyed women got insurance during their pregnancy and then went back to being uninsured postpartum. This chart shows the percentage range for women's insurance status before, during and after pregnancy.

The Race for Early Prenatal Care

Women who begin prenatal care in the first trimester are more likely to have healthy babies, the CDC reports. But access to early prenatal care is not equal between white, black and hispanic infants. From 2012-2014, a black infant was 24 percent less likely and a hispanic infant was 39 percent less likely than a white infant to have received early prenatal care.

What Keeps Women from Initiating Early Prenatal Care

Insurance is not the only factor that determines whether women initiate prenatal care. This chart shows the survey results from a poll conducted by the Maryland Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring system of 4,548 Maryland mothers who gave birth between January 1, 2009 and December 31, 2011. The women surveyed could select multiple responses for why they would not get early prenatal care.

Low Birthweight in Maryland Counties

The CDC uses the term low birthweight to describe babies who are born weighing less than 5 pounds 8 ounces. Infants with low birthweights have a higher risk of respiratory distress, heart problems, anemia and sleep apnea, the CDC reports. This map shows the percentage of low birthweight births in each Maryland county in 2012.

When Women are Initiating Prenatal Care

Pregnant women who begin prenatal care in the first trimester are less likely to have pregnancy complications and more likely to have healthy babies, the Maryland Department of Health reports. But prenatal care at all stages of pregnancy is important. This chart shows the percentage of women who initiated prenatal care at each stage of pregnancy.
