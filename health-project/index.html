Early Prenatal Care in Maryland Post-ACA

by LUCY MAYNARD
When-or if-expecting mothers get prenatal care impacts the future health of their children.

One of the clinics owned and operated by Pregnancy Aid Centers, Inc. (PAC), a non-profit women's health clinic. Organizations such as the Pregnancy Aid Center are still needed for some pregnant women to get early prenatal care. (Pregnancy Aid Center, Inc.)

MARYLAND - The percentage of women receiving early prenatal care in Maryland increased from 82 percent in 2011 to 83 percent in 2013, despite the fact that the Affordable Care Act requires all insurance plans to cover maternal and newborn care-which includes early prenatal care-state data shows.

This is a negligible increase.

A Maryland Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System survey reported the following statistics for 2013:

  • 20.5 percent of women were uninsured before becoming pregnant.
  • 5.7 percent of women were uninsured during pregnancy.
  • 16 percent of women were uninsured postpartum.

This means a majority of uninsured women were getting health care for all or part of their pregnancy and then going back to being uninsured after their pregnancy.

Women who qualify for Medicaid because of their pregnancy “can retain coverage for about two months postpartum,” Brittany Fowler, deputy director of communications for the Maryland Department of Health, said.

Private and marketplace insurance plans under the Affordable Care Act also only provide postpartum care for eight weeks, according to the Maryland Department of Health.

“This allows the mother to get her two postpartum appointments,” Jelacic said.

The first postpartum appointment is to discuss the delivery, birth control, breastfeeding and to check for infection, Jelacic said.

“The second appointment is to medically clear the patient to return to work or school to be sure they have fully healed and to administer birth control if the patient desires it,” Jelacic said.

But the Affordable Care Act’s required maternal and newborn health care coverage, if women were able to get it, ends after these two months, the Maryland Department of Health reported.

Majority of Uninsured Women Who Got Insurance While Pregnant Go Back To Being Uninsured Postpartum

A survey conducted by the Maryland Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System found 20.5 percent of women were uninsured before pregnancy. A majority of the surveyed women got insurance during their pregnancy and then went back to being uninsured postpartum.

The minimal uptick in the percentage of women getting early prenatal care since the Affordable Care Act was passed is compounded by the fact that access to early prenatal care is not equal between races, the March of Dimes Perinatal Data Center reported.

The March of Dimes Perinatal Data Center published the following statistics for Maryland in 2014:

  • 79 percent of live-birth white infants received early prenatal care.
  • 60.3 percent of live-birth black babies received early prenatal care.
  • 52.3 percent of live-birth Hispanic babies received early prenatal care.

This means a black infant was 24 percent less likely and a Hispanic infant was 39 percent less likely than a white infant to have received early prenatal care.

“Many pregnant women do not get the care they need when they need it,” Michele Kling, a March of Dimes spokesperson said. “[This can be the case for] women of all races and ethnicities and from all walks of life.”

Because of socioeconomic factors, some women are less likely to get the care they need, particularly black and Hispanic women, Kling said.

White Infants Are More Likely To Have Received Early Prental Care

Women who begin prenatal care in the first trimester are more likely to have healthy babies, the CDC reports. But access to early prenatal care is not equal between white, black and Hispanic infants. From 2012-2014, a black infant was 24 percent less likely and a Hispanic infant was 39 percent less likely than a white infant to have received early prenatal care.

Maryland expanded Medicaid under the Affordable Care Act, and this expansion “was funded almost entirely by the federal government,” Michel Boudreaux, assistant professor of health services administration at the University of Maryland School of Public Health, said.

Pregnant women can only qualify for Medicaid if they make under a certain income, Laurie Jelacic, assistant director for the Pregnancy Aid Center, a non-profit women’s health clinic, said.

“To help more women have coverage during pregnancy, income requirements for Medicaid are much higher for pregnant women then for women who are not pregnant,” Jelacic said.

But organizations such as the Pregnancy Aid Center are still needed for some pregnant women to get early prenatal care.

“What sets us apart from other OB/GYNs or clinics in the area is we will see patients if their Medicaid is pending or before it [their Medicaid card] says eligible’,” Jelacic said.

An application for Medicaid can take several weeks to process, “which can impede getting into care,” Jelacic said.

“However, the health outcomes of mothers and infants is very complex and involves many factors other than health insurance,” Boudreaux said.

The Maryland Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System surveyed 4,548 Maryland mothers who gave birth between January 1, 2009 and December 31, 2011 about the factors that contributed to them getting late or no prenatal care.

No insurance ranked eighth on the list-behind such factors as no transportation, no leave and not knowing they were pregnant.

Insurance Isn't the Only Thing Keeping Women from Initiating Early Prenatal Care

Insurance is not the only factor that determines whether women initiate prenatal care. The Child Trends Data Bank reported expecting mothers who don’t receive early prenatal care are three times more likely to give birth to a low birthweight baby.

The CDC uses the term low birthweight to describe babies who are born weighing less than 2500 grams, or 5 pounds, 8 ounces.

Babies born with a low birthweight have a higher risk of complications such as respiratory distress, sleep apnea, heart problems, anemia, hyperactivity disorders and mental development, the Stanford Lucile Packard Children’s Hospital reported.

The Maryland Department of Health defines early prenatal care as receiving care within the first trimester of pregnancy.

Women who begin prenatal care in the first three months of pregnancy are less likely to have pregnancy complications and more likely to have healthy babies, the Maryland Department of Health reported.

Pregnant women who receive late (only in the third trimester of pregnancy) or no prenatal care are more likely to have babies with chronic health problems, the Child Trends Data Bank found in a 2015 study.
